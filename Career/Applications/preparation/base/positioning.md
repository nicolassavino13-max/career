## 30-second version (screening opener / "quick intro")

> I'm a McKinsey Financial Services associate coming out of strategy consulting in LatAm. Before McKinsey, I did an MBA at IESE and passed all three CFA levels. My consulting work was mostly in fintech and banking — digital wallets, stablecoin payments, payment acquirers, growth strategy. I'm now making the move from advisory into an operator role, specifically in fintech, payments or digital financial services.

## 90-second version (standard "tell me about yourself")

> I'll give you the three-chapter version — it takes about a minute and a half.
>
> **Chapter one: operator.** I started my career in the Merchant Navy — I was an officer and eventually a Captain at Petrobras, running safety-critical vessel operations and leading a 15-person multinational crew. That wasn't slide work. That was real execution, logistics, and leadership under pressure.
>
> **Chapter two: strategy.** I decided to pivot into finance and business. I did the CFA program — Levels I, II and III — and then an MBA at IESE in Barcelona. That brought me to McKinsey, where I spent the last few years in Financial Services, mostly in LatAm. The projects I'm proudest of were in fintech and payments: helping shape a bank-backed digital wallet that launched across 6 Central American markets, designing a stablecoin-based cross-border payments network for a multi-bank LatAm consortium, and working on payment acquirer transformation in Brazil.
>
> **Where I'm going:** I'm now moving from consulting into an operator role in fintech or payments — something where I own outcomes, not just recommendations. I want to be inside the machine, not advising from outside. That's why this role caught my attention.

## 3-minute version (structured panel / second round)

> Before I get into my background, let me say that my story has an unusual arc — I think that's actually central to what makes me useful.
>
> **I started as an operator, not an analyst.** I spent years in the Merchant Navy as a deck officer and then Captain at Petrobras. My classroom was the bridge of a vessel — safety, logistics, crew leadership, high-stakes decisions with incomplete information. Zero tolerance for failure. That gave me something most consultants don't have: I know what execution actually looks like before the slide deck shows up.
>
> **Then I deliberately transitioned into strategy and finance.** I did the full CFA track — all three levels — and then an MBA at IESE Business School in Barcelona, which is where I built my international network and started focusing on financial services. I joined McKinsey as part of the Financial Services practice in LatAm.
>
> **At McKinsey, I focused on fintech and banking.** The work I'm most proud of is in payments and digital finance. I shaped the business case and product roadmap for a bank-backed digital wallet launched across 6 Central American markets in under 2 months. I designed the strategic plan for a multi-bank consortium to launch a USD-pegged stablecoin for cross-border payments in LatAm. I worked on payment acquirer transformation in Brazil. And I've done growth strategy for retail and SME banking clients across the region. These aren't generic strategy projects — they're directly inside the space I want to move into.
>
> **The move I'm making now:** I'm transitioning from advisory to operator. I've spent years helping financial institutions build and transform — now I want to be inside a company doing it, with ownership over outcomes. I have the strategy foundation, the financial rigor, the payments exposure, and I'm building an AI-native toolkit on top of it. I'm looking for a role where I can compound all of that — not just apply it for 3 months and leave.
>
> That's why I'm here. I'd love to hear your perspective on [what this role owns / what the team is building].

## Why leaving consulting

**Short version:**
> I've spent a few years helping companies transform. I've built the analytical muscle and strategic foundation. What I haven't built yet is the operator muscle — owning outcomes, not just recommendations. I'm not leaving because consulting is bad. I'm leaving because I've gotten what I came for and I'm ready for the next chapter.

**If pushed:**
> The honest version: I got into consulting to develop a framework for thinking about businesses. That worked. But at some point you realize that consulting is a product, not a destination. I want to build inside a company — own something that still exists on Monday when the project ends. Fintech and payments is where I want to do that.

**If asked about timing:**
> The timing is deliberate. I've built the credibility, the exposure, the credentials. The market is right. I don't want to wait another 2 years to make a move I've already decided to make.

## Key differentiators (for "what makes you different?")

1. **Real operator foundation** — I led a crew before I ever made a slide. That's unusual in consulting / strategy profiles.
2. **McKinsey FS + fintech focus** — Not generalist consulting. Payments, digital wallets, stablecoins, acquirers.
3. **Multi-country LatAm** — Cross-border experience with real cultural complexity, not just São Paulo.
4. **CFA III + IESE** — Finance rigor + business school credibility, international network.
5. **Italian/EU passport + trilingual** — Geographic flexibility most local candidates don't have.
6. **AI-native operator** — Building automation workflows, using AI as a productivity multiplier. This is increasingly a moat, not a nice-to-have.

## Handling the "you're just a consultant" objection

> I hear that sometimes. The honest response is: consulting gives you breadth and a framework. It doesn't give you ownership. That's exactly why I'm leaving — because I want the thing consulting doesn't give you. The operator experience I built before consulting is also not typical. I wasn't analyzing operations. I was running them.
