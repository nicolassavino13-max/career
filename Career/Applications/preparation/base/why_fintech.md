# Why Fintech / Why Payments

> Reusable answers for "Why fintech?", "Why payments?", "Why not stay in banking?", "Why now?"

---

## Why fintech (core answer)

**Short:**
> Fintech is where the infrastructure of financial services is being rebuilt. Everything I worked on at McKinsey — wallets, stablecoins, acquirers, digital banking — pointed to the same place: the legacy rails are being replaced and the people building the new ones are in fintech, not in traditional banks.

**Full version:**
> My consulting work kept bringing me back to the same intersection: how financial services companies deal with the fact that the underlying infrastructure — payments, wallets, lending rails, custody — is being rebuilt from scratch. I worked on a stablecoin cross-border payments network, a digital wallet MVP, and payment acquirer transformation. All three of those stories are about the same underlying shift: the digitization of financial infrastructure.
>
> I don't want to be a consultant to that shift. I want to be inside a company that's driving it. That's the move I'm making.

---

## Why payments specifically

> Payments is the foundation. Before you can do digital lending, embedded finance, crypto custody — you need the payment rail. It's the infrastructure layer. Companies like Adyen, Revolut, Stripe — they're building the pipes that everything else runs on. I find that genuinely interesting, and my work history connects to it directly.

---

## Why not stay in traditional banking

> Traditional banking is where financial services used to be built. The talent and innovation cycles have moved. I want to be at a company where the product is digital, the growth is global, and the operating model is lean. That's fintech, not a branch network.

**If the company is a bank (BTG, JPM, etc.):**
> What attracts me here specifically is the transformation / digital mandate — not the traditional banking model. The work you're doing on [operations transformation / digital banking / payments] is closer to what a fintech would do than what most banks do. That's the distinction I care about.

---

## Why now (transition timing)

> The timing is a deliberate choice. I've spent years building the strategy foundation and getting the exposure. I could stay in consulting another 2 years and get the same thing. Instead I'd rather take that foundation and apply it inside a company — while I still have the energy and optionality to make the move count.

---

## Why leaving consulting to join fintech (combined)

> Three reasons. One: the work I want to do — digital products, payments infrastructure, fintech strategy — is done inside companies, not on consulting engagements. Two: I want ownership of outcomes, not temporary advisory access. Three: I've been close to fintech as a consultant. The best career move is to close that last gap and get inside.

---

## Handling "You don't have direct fintech operator experience"

> You're right that my experience has been on the advisory side of fintech, not inside a company. What I do bring is deep exposure to the problems: I've built the models, shaped the strategy, mapped the regulatory path. I know how these products get built and what breaks. The translation from advisory to operator is what I'm asking you to take a bet on — and it's the bet I'm most committed to making work.

---

## Why AI-native matters in this transition

> I'm also building on top of the strategy foundation a genuine AI-native operator toolkit — automated research, workflow design, agentic tools for analysis and execution. This isn't about being an engineer. It's about being the kind of operator who compounds their effectiveness using technology. I think this is increasingly important in strategy, BizOps and GTM roles and most people are not investing in it systematically.
